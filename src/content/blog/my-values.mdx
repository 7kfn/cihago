# My Values

## What is a value? What isn't? 

Sit down when a friend or a family member and ask them what their values are? Or better, ask yourself what your values are. 

Typically, the answers come back in three broad categories:

1. Ambitions or goals e.g. 'Being a hard working person'
2. Items or feelings that are important to the person and they want to hold on to e.g. 'my family'
3. Generic 'good' statements, occasionally about politics and often inter-personal. e.g. 'treat others how you would like to be treated'

In an ACT framework, none of these are values. They're certainly not worthless and are important in many other ways but they're not a value. 

> A value is a direction, not a destination. It's an aspirational way-of-doing or way-of-being.

The clearest entry-point for thinking about values is Virtue Ethics and the best reference I know of is a booked called Nichomedian Ethics. It's a collection of oral lectures, given by Aristotle to the sons of wealthy Athenian patricians. Despite this highfalutin context, these lectures are better understood as the Self Help genre of the ancient world. The name 'ethics' is confusing, far outdating our modern use of the word 'ethics' and instead concerning the Greek word ἠθική - much closer to 'character' or 'disposition' then 'moral fibre'. 

Without getting lost in the book, Aristotle urges the young men to develop 'eudimonia' - usually translated to 'flourishing' - by building and practing virtious habits. How should you know what a 'virtious habit' is? Well, it's something that comes from being guided by some combinition of values, values like moderation, bravery, thrift, ambition etc. 

Already from this, and from an inspection of the plain English understanding of values, we can see the following key criteria emerging: 

1. Enduring. A value should continue to be your value whether or not your live up to it. For Aristotle, the man who has achieved eudimonia still continues to express those values. 
2. Diserning, or guiding. A doesn't necessarily need to be hard, but it needs to discern. Some actions or habits should fulfil that value while others do not. It's not trivial[^1].
3. Intrinsic, or autotelic. One value does not supervene on another. A good value needs no further justification and thus, is virtually impossible ot quation from the outside (other than from a systems perspective, and even then may still respond 'yes my values are in tension, so what?')

Ambitions and goals are not enduring - they are achieved or failed. If you find yourself listing ambitions as your values, consider what your next ambition might be after you achieved or failed the current one. What ties those two ambitions together. 

Things of value are also not enduring, and they offer no guidance. Soemtimes theose ideas can be ported accross to values easily through 'protection of' or 'enhancement of'. For example 'I value my family' becomes 'Protecting my family' - but you would want to deeper to build in more guidance. What do you want to protect about them? What would it mean to protect your family if you didn't have the same family? 

Many truisms are _technically_ values but they're functionally useless because they lack any kind of discerning quality. 'Be a good person'. Thicken the adjective - what is good? 

## Why do they matter? 

Values already matter to you, whether you like it or not, because they are the foundation of the most important qualities in your personality - willpower, impulse control, agency. 

Of course, it's possible to summon up willpower or generate agency but this process is limited, exhausting and contextual. It's downstream of how much sleep you had, what you ate, the weather and any number of factors. 

On the other hand, if you are acting downstream of your values then it _can_ become effortless and clear. 

Take a modern example that is close to many people my age - 'I want to spend less time on my phone'. This is half an ambition, half a generic statement. The typical approach here would be to wake up in the morning, pick up your phone as usual and then _will yourself_ into putting it down and doing something else. 

But, the next day, perhaps you argued with your partner or had a bad day at work. It no longer seems that important to spend less time on your phone and you're stressed, so you give yourself a break and promise to pick it up again. 

Contrast that with a values-based approach. What is the value that underlies wanting to spend less time on your phone? For example, it might be something simple like 'Enjoy my free time more' and you could flesh that out to 'Feeling enhanced and refreshed by my downtime'.

It's now irrevelant whether you stayed off your phone yesterday, or if you had a bad day and you no longer care about staying off your phone. You can be doomscrolling and ask yourself - 'am I feeling refreshed?' Of course, the answer might be 'yes'! But, more often than not, the answer will be 'no'. 

## My Values

* Adventure
    * Adventures have risk that can't be captured, weighed or moved on to someone else. The risk has to be real. 
    * Adventures are exploring something new. 
* Relative Ambition
    * I'm trying to get better and I don't settle for 'good enough' 
    * Why have less when you can have move? Why have something be good when it could be great? More is more.
    * Relative
        * That means that I'm trying to get better from where I am at any one time. I don't need to be the best runner in the world (excellence), but I want to be a better runner tomorrow than I am today. 
        * The time-period is flexible here. There is Brownian motion in the path so not every day needs to be better than every yesterday but my value is that, all things being equal, it's better for tomorrow to be an improvement on today. 
* Getting the best out of other people - Enhance
    * I want to see those around me who are close to me, and it some extend those not close to me, do well and succeed and be great people. 
    * I'm not here to tell them how to live their life but I can make them great in all sorts of ways. They can decide what is best for them.
* “Get Tough” - Exert
    * Putting your head down occasionally and doing it the hard way
    * Being able to take the pain and the misery 
    * Not whinging
    * Having tough conversations
* Integrity - Own
    * "I carry my own bag"
        * I pull (at least) my weight. I don't ask others to do it for me. I don't ask other people to carry my bag for me other than in very exceptional circumstances. 
        * Sometimes I do more than my fair share because I can.
        * This isn't just 'mucking in' or 'just doing my bit' - it's about not asking other people to carry the load that I'm meant to be carrying. I'm not a shirker. 
    * Speak The Truth
        * Say the thing you think is true 
        * Don’t say stuff you don’t think isn’t true 
    * "Doing What Works"
        * I'm trying to take the actions and hold the beliefs that actually work - that is, they achieve the ends they’re intended to achieve. 
        * That means being evidence driven, and learning from experience. Not doing things because they've always been done that way. 
        * If something isn't working - Stop doing it. 
    * Agency 
        * "I am the captain of my ship, I am the master of my soul' 
        * I really don't want to be under the spell, under the influence of someone else or something else. 
* Looking after myself - Anchor
    * "Put your own life jacket on before putting on someone else’s'
    * If you aren't in good working order - in whatever sense - then how can you peruse your other values?
* Fun - Enjoy
    * Life is for living 
    * Have fun, laugh, smoke the cigarette, enjoy yourself
    * Do things you think are funny - perhaps only because you find them funny
